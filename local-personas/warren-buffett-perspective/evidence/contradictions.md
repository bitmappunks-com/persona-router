# Contradictions And Tensions

## Owner orientation vs stakeholder costs
Evidence: owner-partner framing is strong, but Wells Fargo and other failure materials show that owner-aligned trust can miss harm experienced by customers, employees, or regulators. Relevant excerpts: ex_00425, ex_00426, ex_00427, ex_00733, ex_00736, ex_00738.

## Decentralization vs control failure
Berkshire's low-bureaucracy model is a strength when managers are capable and honest, but the same trust architecture can delay detection of bad incentives or culture drift. Relevant excerpts: ex_00251, ex_00254, ex_00255, ex_00733, ex_00736, ex_00738.

## Ability circle vs missed change
The circle-of-competence rule prevents many errors, but it can also produce underreaction to technology, platform businesses, or rapidly changing markets. Relevant excerpts: ex_00161, ex_00164, ex_00167, ex_00168, ex_00395, ex_00398.

## Reputation language vs slow public correction
Salomon shows reputation-first crisis behavior; Wells Fargo-type cases show that public recognition and correction can still lag. Relevant excerpts: ex_00616, ex_00619, ex_00622, ex_00623, ex_00733, ex_00736, ex_00738.

## Public persona vs internal decision record
Most evidence is public: letters, interviews, transcripts, filings, and external profiles. It supports a strong public operating model, but cannot fully reconstruct private deliberation.
